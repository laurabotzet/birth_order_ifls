Abstract

Is it possible to replicate effects of birth order on intelligence, personality, and risk preference cross-culturally? So far, studies on birth order effects focused on samples from western, educated, industrialized, rich, democratic (WEIRD) countries. Based on earlier research we stated three hypotheses: (1) Intelligence declines with increasing birth order position, (2) personality is not related to birth order position and (2) risk preference rises with increasing birth order position. The Indonesian Family Life Survey collected data about intelligence, personality, and risk preference in the year 2014. Based on this data and the maternal pregnancy history we were able to determine birth order position, family membership and independent variables for 4,666 individuals. Analyses were performed using within-family designs in mixed-effects models. In model comparisons we analyzed the effect of birth order on the independent variables. We tested for linear and non-linear birth order effects as well as for possible interactions of birth order and sibship size. In contrast to earlier WEIRD-research we found a linear increase of intelligence with increasing birth order. Birth order had a categorical effect on extraversion and openness but not on neuroticism, conscientiousness, nor aggreeableness. The interaction of birth order and sibship size was significant for one risk preference measurement. We did not replicate results from earlier studies on WEIRD-people. This evidence points towards social mechanisms behind possible birth order effects based on cultural, political and economic circumstances rather than universally valid, biological mechanisms.
